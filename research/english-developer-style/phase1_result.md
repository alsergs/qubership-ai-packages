# Pass 1 Discovery: Sources for an LLM Skill That Writes Natural Developer-Facing English

## 1. Executive summary

No single proven, off-the-shelf LLM skill exists that targets the exact problem: writing and editing developer-facing English prose that avoids LLM stylistic tells while honouring British-English defaults and software-doc conventions. The Agent Skills ecosystem (skills.sh, Anthropic's `anthropics/skills`, `obra/superpowers`, `ComposioHQ/awesome-claude-skills`, Mintlify's `skill.md`) is large and growing fast, but the writing-oriented entries in it are dominated by general "content writer", "copy editor", "README writer" and marketing-flavoured skills. None I found combines (a) British-English defaults, (b) explicit LLM-tell mitigation, and (c) software-doc scope.

The strongest source category is **editorial style guides for software documentation** (Google, Microsoft, GitLab, Red Hat/IBM, Kubernetes, Atlassian, DigitalOcean, Write the Docs). These are mature, named-author, actively maintained and concretely applicable to developer prose. The second strongest is **prose linters and their rule catalogues** (Vale plus the Google/Microsoft/IBM/write-good/proselint/alex packages, and proselint itself), because they already encode rules in a portable, near-skill-ready form.

The weakest and most polluted category is **prompt libraries and "AI humanizer" advice**: most hits optimise for AI-detector evasion rather than reader experience, and they conflict with the user's explicit framing. Cursor/Continue-style rule packs are also weak for this purpose — they exist in volume but almost all target code generation, not prose editing.

British-English-specific authoritative sources are available but thinner than US ones. GOV.UK (Government Digital Service) is the standout, supported by the Guardian and Observer style guide, New Hart's Rules / the New Oxford Style Manual, and (less reliably) older BBC and Economist guides. None is software-specific; each must be combined with a US-anchored developer-doc guide.

**Recommendation for Pass 2 focus:** combine. Lead with editorial style guides (for principles and word lists) and Vale rule sets (for portable, near-mechanical checks). Layer LLM-tell analyses on top (for the failure modes the style guides do not cover). Treat agent-skill repos and prompt libraries as pattern references for *packaging* the skill, not as content sources.

## 3. Promising shortlist for Pass 2

1. **Google Developer Documentation Style Guide** (#1). Promising because it is the de facto baseline for developer-doc voice and word choice, is actively maintained, and is already mirrored in a Vale package. Likely contributes: word list (canonical "click"/"select", "disabled" vs "unavailable"), placeholder-style guidance, code-in-text rules, second-person voice. Most useful as **authoring + editing** content. Converts cleanly into a compact `SKILL.md` because rules are already short and rule-shaped.

2. **Microsoft Writing Style Guide** (#2). Promising as a complement to Google: a different (warmer, contraction-friendly) voice. Likely contributes: contractions guidance, sentence-case headings, "bigger ideas, fewer words", anti-jargon. **Mixed** use. Converts well; pair with #1 to triangulate.

3. **GitLab Documentation Style Guide and Vale rules** (#3). Promising because it is the most rules-as-code example in the wild — already ships Vale YAML for self-referential prose, weak-subject phrases, gerund-avoidance, and a British-English detector. Likely contributes: hedging detection, "this guide explains" anti-pattern, ambiguous-pronoun and -ing-word checks. Strong **linting-or-review** input. Converts very well.

4. **Vale + the errata-ai package set** (#17). Promising because the YAML rules are essentially pre-compressed `SKILL.md` checklists, and the Microsoft/Google/IBM/write-good/proselint/alex/Joblint/Readability packages cover most of the territory. **Linting-or-review** primary use; the catalog can be re-expressed as natural-language editorial rules for an LLM. Converts well, with curation.

5. **Wikipedia: Signs of AI writing** (#21). Promising because it is the most thorough, named, community-curated catalogue of LLM tells, organised by LLM era and with linked examples. Likely contributes: tells beyond em-dashes (rule-of-three, formulaic openings, promotional drift, era-specific vocabulary like "delve"/"tapestry"), useful for the failure-mode side of the skill. Pure **background / editing-or-rewrite**. Converts to a focused "tells to suppress" checklist.

6. **proselint** (#18). Promising because its rules come from named editorial authorities (Garner, Pinker, Strunk, White, Butterick, Corbett) and were peer-reviewed in the SciPy 2016 paper by Pacer and Suchow. Likely contributes: uncomparables, hedge stacking, clichés, "very unique" type rules. **Linting-or-review**. Converts very well; small enough to enumerate.

7. **GOV.UK Style Guide (and its words-to-avoid list)** (#10). Promising as the primary British-English authority and as a plain-language anchor compatible with developer-doc principles. Likely contributes: British spelling defaults, "words to avoid" (deliver, leverage, robust, key, etc.), plain-English thresholds. **Mixed** use. Converts well; small targeted excerpts.

8. **Kubernetes Documentation Style Guide** (#6). Promising because it is the most concrete real-world OSS application of Google's style, with extra rules for API object names, placeholders, and callouts that are directly developer-facing. **Mixed**; demonstrates how rules behave in a large-OSS context. Converts well into examples within the skill.

9. **Atlassian Design System content guidance** (#7). Promising specifically for **error messages, log messages and microcopy** — a domain the other style guides cover only thinly. Likely contributes: tone calibration for failure states, "tell them what they need to know and get out of the way." **Authoring**. Converts to a small voice-and-tone module.

10. **Conventional Commits** (#25). Promising because the skill must cover commit messages and PR descriptions, and Conventional Commits is the canonical structural spec. Likely contributes: commit-message grammar (imperative mood, lowercase verb, type/scope), changelog phrasing. **Authoring**. Converts very well as a small embedded ruleset.

## 4. Obvious rejects

- **"AI humanizer" tools and ChatGPT-watermark removers** (e.g. gpt-watermark-remover, Rumi NNBSP guides, Codeless watermark guides). Reject: they optimise for AI-detector evasion, not reader experience, which the brief explicitly excludes.
- **Grammarly's AI Detector and similar consumer detectors**. Reject: detection-oriented; no editorial rule catalogue useful for authoring.
- **Most entries in `PatrickJS/awesome-cursorrules`**. Reject: overwhelmingly about code generation in specific frameworks; almost no entries target prose. Useful only as packaging precedent.
- **`ComposioHQ/awesome-claude-skills` content-research-writer** and similar "content writer" skills. Reject: marketing/SEO/long-form-article slant, not developer documentation; voice-preservation framing conflicts with the user's brief.
- **Generic `dotcursorrules.com` rule packs and Atlan's blog rules**. Reject: code-focused; no prose-quality rules.
- **Hemingway Editor, LanguageTool, After the Deadline**. Reject (for skill content): grammar-only or readability-only; little editorial style guidance specific to developer docs.
- **BBC News Style Guide via legacy PDFs**. Reject as primary source: canonical hosting is unstable; only third-party mirrors of an older John Allen guide circulate, so editorial provenance is shaky. Keep as a tertiary British reference only.
- **"AI humanizer" prompt lists and Medium "make ChatGPT sound human" articles**. Reject: anonymous, often detector-driven, conflict with the brief.
- **`VoltAgent/awesome-claude-code-subagents` technical-writer agent**. Reject as a content source: it is a role-prompt scaffold, not an editorial ruleset; might be useful as packaging precedent only.

## 5. Gaps and questions for Pass 2

1. **Worked examples**: Google, Microsoft and GitLab guides have many before/after examples. Does Wikipedia's Signs of AI writing offer enough concrete bad-prose examples to compile a high-quality "tells" appendix? Are the proselint rules' false-positive rates documented well enough to choose which to import?
2. **British vs US conflicts**: Where do Google/Microsoft style and GOV.UK/Guardian style materially disagree (single vs double quotation marks, sentence-final punctuation inside vs outside quotes, "ize/ise", Oxford comma)? The skill must resolve these explicitly because the user wants British default.
3. **False-positive complaints**: Vale issues and proselint issues should be sampled to check which rules produce stilted prose when applied mechanically (the user's brief flags this explicitly). Are there known weak rules to skip — e.g. write-good's passive-voice rule, which is widely criticised?
4. **Coverage of failure modes beyond em-dashes**: Do Wikipedia's Signs of AI writing, the "LLM Prose Tells" doc, and the Freeburg em-dash paper between them cover the full baseline (mechanically balanced sentences, "it's not X, it's Y", colon elaboration, paragraph-closing summary, formulaic openings) with citable evidence?
5. **British-English support in Vale**: GitLab's `British.yml` is one example. Are there richer British-English Vale rule sets in the Vale Package Explorer? Pass 2 should search specifically.
6. **Compressibility into `SKILL.md`**: Google's word list alone is hundreds of entries. What is the right cut-down — frequency-weighted to developer-doc usage, or topic-targeted (placeholders, UI verbs, error-state vocabulary, version-history phrasing)?
7. **Contradictions with developer-doc style**: Does the user's baseline (e.g. strict em-dash prohibition, gerund after "rather than") align with Google/Microsoft/GitLab guidance, or extend it? Specifically check Google's allowance of em-dashes for examples ("Enter a name — for example, my-instance-99") and DigitalOcean's explicit em-dash in step headers; the baseline conflicts here and must take a deliberate position.
8. **Error-message and log-message prose**: Atlassian and Microsoft are strongest, but is there a dedicated, citable error-message guide (Nielsen Norman, Apple HIG, GitLab error-message subsection) that should be added in Pass 2?
9. **Skill packaging precedent**: Should the skill follow Anthropic's `anthropics/skills` shape, `obra/superpowers`' TDD-for-skills shape, or the Mintlify/agentskills.io spec? Pass 2 should compare the three concretely against the rule-density of this domain.
10. **Maintenance signal**: For each shortlisted source, log the last meaningful update date and cadence. Google's What's New page shows multiple distinct April 2026 entries (sub-monthly cadence); IBM book is frozen at 2011; GOV.UK is being replaced by the GOV.UK content and publishing guidance. The skill should preferentially encode rules from sources with active editorial review.
